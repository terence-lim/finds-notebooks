FOMC meeting minutes#

The FOMC holds eight regularly scheduled meetings during the year and other meetings as needed. The minutes of regularly scheduled meetings are released three weeks after the date of the policy decision. They provide insights into the Federal Reserve’s economic outlook and potential future monetary policy actions, which can impact interest rates, inflation, and market conditions.

https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomccalendars.htm

# Helper function to trim minutes text
def edit(text: str) -> str:
    """helper to spawn editor and write/edit/read to tempfile"""
    import subprocess
    import tempfile
    with tempfile.NamedTemporaryFile(suffix=".tmp") as f: # save temp file
        f.write(text.encode("utf-8"))
        f.flush()
        subprocess.call([os.environ.get('EDITOR','emacs'), "-nw", f.name])
        f.seek(0)
        return f.read().decode("utf-8")        # keep edited text

Text pre-processing#

Text pre-processing involves cleaning and preparing raw text data by removing noise, standardizing formats, and converting text into a structured format suitable for analysis. The first phase of textual analysis, it involves some or all the steps of tokenizating, removing stop words, casing, and vectorizing.

Tokenization#

Split text into smaller units called tokens, which are usually words, but also may be phrases or subwords. The text pattern of tokens and token boundaries are often expressed using the language of regular expressions.

Regular expressions#

A regular expression is a special text spring for describing a text search pattern to match character combinations in strings. They are used in tokenization, to find word boundaries and remove whitespace.

Stopwords#

Uninformative common words can be removed.

Casing#

The original casing of words may be retained if the distinction between uppercase and lowercase letters carries important semantic or syntactic information, such as differentiating proper nouns, acronyms, or the beginnings of sentences.

Note that some models may interpret all upper case words as organisations.

# ignore these stop words

Vectorization#

Converts text into numerical representations

  • Bag-of-words: In a BOW approach, a documents is represented by the frequency of its words, disregarding grammar and word order but preserving multiplicity.

  • N-grams: Two (or more) consecutive words can be treated as distinct items, not just isolated words.

  • Indexing: Unique words in the corpus are mapped to consecutive integer indexes, forming the vocabulary.

  • TF-IDF: Term-frequency Inverse-document-frequency vectorization transforms text into numerical features by measuring the importance of a word in a document relative to its frequency across all documents in a corpus. The scikit-learn package provides the TfidfVectorizaer for vectorizing text with the TF-IDF technique, as well as CountVectorizer which simply counts the raw number of word occurences.

# To vectorize the input words

Topic modeling#

Topic modeling uses statistical techniques to discover latent topics that exist within a collection of documents.

Latent Semantic Analysis#

LSA uses singular value decomposition to analyze the relationships between terms and documents in a corpus. It constructs a matrix of the document term vectors, then through dimensionality reduction identifies underlying topics by grouping together terms and documents that co-occur frequently in this reduced space.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation#

LDA assumes that each document is a mixture of topics, and each topic is a mixture of words. It iteratively assigns words in documents to topics probabilistically based on the distribution of topics in the document and the distribution of words in the topic.

Non-negative matrix factorization#

NMF decomposes a term-document matrix into two lower-dimensional non-negative matrices: a basis matrix representing topics and a coefficient matrix representing the distribution of topics in documents. By iteratively optimizing these matrices to best reconstruct the original term-document matrix, NMF identifies latent topics and their distributions across documents.

Probabilistic Latent Semantic Indexing#

PLSI assumes that documents are generated from a mixture of topics, and words within documents are generated based on these topics. It estimates the probability distributions of topics and words, iteratively refining these distributions until convergence. It can be implemented by NMF, with generalized Kullback-Leibler divergence as the norm.

  • https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/decomposition.html#nmf

  • https://scikit-learn.org/stable/auto_examples/applications/plot_topics_extraction_with_nmf_lda.html

# Define models
n_components = 4   # fix number of latent topics
algos = {
    'LSA': (TruncatedSVD(n_components=n_components),
            tfidf_vectorizer),
    'LDA': (LatentDirichletAllocation(n_components=n_components,
                                      learning_method='batch', #'online',
                                      # learning_offset = 50.0,
                                      max_iter = 40,
                                      random_state = 42),
            tf_vectorizer),
    'PLSI': (NMF(n_components=n_components,
                 beta_loss='kullback-leibler',
                 solver='mu', 
                 alpha_W=0.00005,
                 alpha_H=0.00005,
                 l1_ratio=0.5,
                 max_iter=1000,
                 random_state = 42),
             tfidf_vectorizer),
    'NMF': (NMF(n_components=n_components,
                random_state=42,
                beta_loss='frobenius',
                alpha_W=0.00005,
                alpha_H=0.00005,
                l1_ratio=0.5),
            tfidf_vectorizer)}

Fit the models, and plot the predicted topics by date of document

# Fit and plot models
scores = dict()   # to save model coefficients
topics = dict()   # to save dates of primary topic
for ifig, (name, (base, vectorizer)) in enumerate(algos.items()):

    # vectorize the input words
    vectorized = vectorizer.fit_transform(docs.to_list())
    feature_names = vectorizer.get_feature_names_out()

    # fit model and transform inputs
    model = base.fit(vectorized)
    transformed = model.transform(vectorized)

    # save the model fitted coefficients
    if name == 'LSA':  # Additional step for LSA
        kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=n_components, n_init=5, random_state=37)\
            .fit(transformed)    # find centroids of the latent factors
        transformed = kmeans.transform(transformed)  # distance to centroid
        transformed = -(transformed / transformed.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True))
        scores[name] = softmax(model.components_, axis=1)  # scale word scores
    else:
        scores[name] = model.components_

    # plot topic scores over time
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(num=1 + ifig, clear=True, figsize=(10, 6))
    dates = pd.DatetimeIndex(docs.index.astype(str))        
    ax.step(dates, transformed, where='pre')
    for a,b in vspans:
        if b >= min(dates):
            ax.axvspan(a, min(b, max(dates)), alpha=0.3, color='grey')
    ax.set_title(name)
    ax.legend([f"{i+1}" for i in range(n_components)] + ['Recession'],
              loc='center left', title='Topic score')
    plt.tight_layout(pad=2)

    # save dates of primary topic
    for topic in range(transformed.shape[1]):
        arg = DataFrame({'t': np.argmax(transformed, axis=1),
                         'dt': docs.index})
        dates = (arg!=arg.shift()).cumsum().groupby('t').agg(['first', 'last']) - 1
        dates['topic'] = arg.loc[dates.iloc[:,1], 't'].values
        topics[name] = {topic: [(arg['dt'].iloc[row[0]], arg['dt'].iloc[row[1]])
                                for row in dates.itertuples(index=False, name=None)
                                if row[2] == topic]
                        for topic in range(transformed.shape[1])}
